Published February 21, 2026 · Source file analyzed: holidays.json in this repository.

Executive Summary

  • Animals & Nature (37.16%) and Food (34.43%) account for 71.59% of all 732 records.

Category Dominance Analysis

What This Means for Content and Creators

The catalog is top-heavy. Animals & Nature (272 records, 37.16%) and Food (252 records, 34.43%) account for more than 71% of all entries combined. Arts & Culture is a clear third tier at 91 records (12.43%), while all remaining categories collectively represent roughly 16% of the dataset. For editorial operations, this means high-volume discovery opportunities are concentrated in only a few thematic lanes.

The top three dominant categories are:

  • Animals & Nature – 272 records (37.16%)
  • Food – 252 records (34.43%)
  • Arts & Culture – 91 records (12.43%)

This composition supports a two-track strategy: volume capture in dominant lanes, and differentiation in mid-share lanes such as Arts & Culture and Wellness & Lifestyle. It also explains why users often enter through broad-interest pages and then branch into narrower categories via internal links.

Food Holiday Analysis

What This Means for Editorial Planning

Food-related holidays represent 34.43% of the full dataset (252 of 732 entries), making food the second-largest category and one of the strongest repeat-use content pools. To make this actionable, the food set was further split into subtypes using deterministic keyword rules on holiday names. This subtype view is useful for publishers deciding whether to focus on recipe-friendly, beverage-friendly, or seasonal comfort-food narratives.

The largest food subtype in this snapshot is Other Food (106 entries, 42.06% of food records), followed by Dessert & Sweets (84 entries, 33.33%). Savory Meals contributes 27 entries (10.71%), Beverages contributes 22 entries (8.73%), and Produce & Ingredients contributes 13 entries (5.16%). This split shows that food demand in the catalog is not exclusively dessert-driven; there is enough savory and beverage inventory to support diverse editorial planning.

“Food-related holidays account for 34.43% of all records, with 57.94% in non-dessert subtypes.”

For internal linking strategy, this subtype spread can support tighter “food lane” navigation: dessert clusters for seasonal baking periods, beverage clusters for quick social formats, and savory clusters for utility-first recipe content.

Animal vs Awareness Breakdown

What This Means for Topic Balance

The difference between celebratory topics and advocacy-style topics is substantial. Animals & Nature holds 272 entries, while Awareness & Advocacy holds 7. That is a 38.9 to 1 ratio in favor of celebratory and affinity-based observances. In percentage terms, Animals & Nature accounts for 37.16% of the entire catalog, versus 0.96% for awareness-focused entries.

This gap is strategically relevant. If the product goal is broad engagement, the existing distribution already favors high-share themes that perform well in lightweight social and evergreen formats. If the goal includes educational or public-interest depth, awareness content is currently underrepresented and may require intentional expansion policies. The point is not that one lane is “better,” but that the current inventory significantly skews toward celebratory discovery across pages like Animals & Nature.

“The Animals & Nature category is 38.9 times larger than Awareness & Advocacy in the current dataset.”

The Obscurity Index

What This Means for Long-Tail Search

The Obscurity Index in this report is a practical proxy built from name-level specificity rather than subjective scoring. It combines three signals: longest holiday names, niche-themed wording, and specific hobby-oriented themes. Longer names often indicate narrower intent or higher lexical specificity, while niche and hobby terms identify topics that are less likely to be discovered through broad category browsing.

Longest names in the current snapshot include “National Pizza with the Works Except Anchovies Day” (50 characters), “Answer The Telephone Like Buddy The Elf Day” (43), and “National Law Enforcement Appreciation Day” (41). These titles are structurally specific and can work as strong long-tail landing pages when paired with contextual internal links.

Niche-themed examples include terms such as Morse code, cellophane tape, plimsoll, and barbershop quartet. Hobby-related examples include photography, puzzle, kite flying, museum, dance, and reading-oriented observances. In aggregate, these subsets are smaller than top-level category lanes but useful for expertise signals, newsletter segmentation, and partner campaigns that need precision over volume, especially when linked from broader clusters like Seasonal Holidays.

Top 5 Surprising Insights

Fast Stats for Journalists and Editors

  1. Two categories dominate discovery. Animals & Nature and Food together account for 71.59% of records, concentrating search and navigation demand in two lanes.
  2. Awareness content is structurally underrepresented. At 0.96% share, Awareness & Advocacy is much smaller than most commercial and celebratory categories.
  3. Food breadth is wider than expected. Non-dessert food entries (other food, savory, beverages, produce) represent 57.94% of food holidays.
  4. Top-three concentration is unusually high. The top three categories account for 84.02%, indicating strong content clustering effects.
  5. Niche naming is a real long-tail asset. The longest and most specific holiday names create natural opportunities for highly targeted queries and coverage.
“The top three categories capture 84.02% of all records, leaving only 15.98% for the remaining five categories.”

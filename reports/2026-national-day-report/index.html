The 2026 National Day Report: 756 Holidays Analyzed

Published February 21, 2026 · Data source: holidays.json snapshot in repository.

1. Intro: A Data-Driven View of the National Day Calendar

This report is designed as a linkable data asset, not a narrative blog post. It translates the Obscure Holiday Calendar dataset into a clear structural view of how national day observances are distributed across the year, where concentration actually happens, and which topical categories dominate the calendar. For transparency: the current holidays.json snapshot contains 732 dated holiday records. The title uses the project campaign label requested for this report, while all metrics below are computed from the live dataset file in this repository.

Three practical questions drive the analysis. First, is the calendar seasonally dense in ways that content teams can plan around? Second, how much of the catalog is concentrated in a few high-volume categories versus long-tail themes? Third, are there specific dates that are truly crowded, or is density relatively uniform across the year? The outputs include monthly counts, category share, and date-level rankings. Because this is intended for journalists, creators, and marketers, each section focuses on interpretable numbers rather than abstract scoring.

2. Holiday Density by Month

Monthly density is highly stable. Counts range from 58 to 62, and the variance is driven mostly by month length rather than seasonal campaign effects. January, March, May, July, August, October, and December each hold 62 entries. February has 58, while April, June, September, and November each hold 60. This matters because teams often assume an uneven seasonal landscape; in this dataset, the baseline inventory is structurally consistent, which supports year-round planning.

Operationally, this means a publication calendar does not need to over-concentrate only around Q4 or major retail periods. There is enough date-level opportunity every month to sustain campaigns, lesson plans, or editorial cadences without forcing off-topic picks. From an internal linking perspective, monthly stability also supports predictable crawl pathways from month hubs to day-specific pages.

3. Category Breakdown (with Percentages)

Category inference on holiday names and descriptions shows two dominant segments: animals & nature (37.16%) and food (34.43%). Together they account for over 71% of all entries, indicating that practical discovery demand is likely to cluster around lifestyle-friendly themes that are visually shareable and easy to activate in short-form content. Arts & culture contributes 12.43%, while wellness & lifestyle contributes 5.33%. Seasonal entries are only 3.28%, confirming that broad calendar opportunity is not limited to traditional holiday moments.

For stakeholders, this mix implies clear editorial lane design. If your goal is reach, food and animals/nature are high-supply domains with predictable recurrence. If your goal is differentiation, arts/culture and wellness offer lower-volume but often higher-context storytelling opportunities. Category hubs can be used to balance breadth and depth: broad categories for traffic capture, narrower categories for expertise signals and repeat audience behavior.

4. Most Crowded Calendar Dates

The top date ranking reveals an important structural property: crowding is flat. The top ten dates all have two holidays, and the same two-per-day pattern holds across the calendar in this snapshot. This is a useful finding for newsroom and campaign planning, because it means “competition pressure” by date is not the main differentiator. Selection quality, relevance fit, and supporting context are more likely to drive performance than choosing a less crowded day.

In practical terms, teams should optimize around audience intent and thematic alignment, not just date scarcity. If every date has similar inventory, better outcomes come from stronger packaging: concise explainers, related-link pathways, and media-ready visuals.

5. Fastest Growing / Notable Categories

This snapshot does not include historical versions with category timestamps, so true year-over-year growth rates cannot be computed from a single file without introducing assumptions. What is derivable is structural prominence and editorial leverage. By that standard, animals/nature and food are the notable categories due to both share and continuity across months. Arts/culture is the most actionable mid-tier category because it is large enough for recurring programming but not saturated enough to force repetitive angles.

For category strategy, use a tiered model. Tier 1 (animals/nature + food) supports reach and recurring engagement. Tier 2 (arts/culture + wellness/lifestyle) supports brand voice and differentiation. Tier 3 (seasonal, awareness, work/technology) supports campaign spikes and topical partnerships. The linked hubs below are useful entry points for that model: Arts & Culture, Wellness & Lifestyle, Animals & Nature, and Seasonal.

6. Why This Matters for Creators, Educators, and Brands

For creators, the report provides planning certainty. Stable monthly density means you can run an “always-on” publishing model without exhausting relevant dates. For educators, the category split helps map observances to lesson formats: science and nature tie-ins for younger students, arts and culture for literacy projects, and wellness prompts for social-emotional learning activities. For brands, the data supports portfolio planning: broad categories for volume, focused categories for niche authority, and seasonal categories for campaign timing.

The secondary value is editorial defensibility. Instead of choosing observances ad hoc, teams can cite measured category share and date structure when prioritizing pages, campaigns, or partnerships. That improves consistency and reduces the churn that happens when content calendars are built around isolated viral moments rather than durable inventory.

7. Methodology

  • Source file: repository holidays.json snapshot analyzed on 2026-02-21.
  • Total rows counted: 732 holiday entries.
  • Monthly totals: grouped by MM from the MM-DD key.
  • Top crowded dates: ranked by holiday count per MM-DD (descending, then chronological tie-break).
  • Category percentages: keyword-based inference on holiday name + description, then primary-category assignment for each row.
  • Charts are static SVG assets generated from computed values; no manual value entry.

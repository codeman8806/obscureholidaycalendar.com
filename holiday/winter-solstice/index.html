Winter Solstice ❄

December 21 Cultural / community observance Updated 2025-12-07

This holiday is featured in the Obscure Holiday Calendar app with artwork, reminders, and daily fun facts.

Overview

The Winter Solstice marks the shortest day and longest night of the year in the Northern Hemisphere, occurring when the North Pole is tilted furthest away from the Sun. It is an astronomical event that signifies the official start of winter, after which the days gradually begin to lengthen.

Observed each year on December 21, Winter Solstice invites people to pause, share the story, and bring a little themed joy to their day.

Origin and story

The Winter Solstice has been celebrated since ancient times by various cultures, often associated with rebirth, new beginnings, and the return of light.

In many cultures, the Winter Solstice is known by different names, such as Yule in Germanic traditions or Dongzhi Festival in East Asia.

Fun facts

  • The Winter Solstice has been celebrated since ancient times by various cultures, often associated with rebirth, new beginnings, and the return of light.
  • In many cultures, the Winter Solstice is known by different names, such as Yule in Germanic traditions or Dongzhi Festival in East Asia.
  • Stonehenge in England is famously aligned with the solstices, with its central axis pointing to the sunrise on the winter solstice.
  • Though it's the shortest day, the coldest temperatures typically occur weeks or months after the Winter Solstice due to thermal inertia.
  • The Earth's orbit around the sun causes the solstices. The winter solstice occurs when the sun reaches its southernmost point in the sky, directly overhead at the Tropic of Capricorn.
  • It is a moment when many animals, like bears, enter hibernation or significantly reduce their activity.

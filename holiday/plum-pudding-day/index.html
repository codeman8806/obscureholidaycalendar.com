Overview

Plum Pudding Day, observed annually on February 12th, is an unofficial holiday celebrating the rich, steamed dessert traditionally served during the Christmas season in Britain and other Commonwealth countries. Despite its name, traditional plum pudding (also known as Christmas pudding) typically contains no plums; the term "plum" historically referred to raisins, currants, and other dried fruits that form the bulk of its dense, flavorful consistency. This festive dessert is often prepared weeks in advance and served flaming, usually doused in brandy, as a grand finale to a holiday meal.

Observed each year on February 12, Plum Pudding Day invites people to pause, share the story, and bring a little themed joy to their day.

Origin and story

Despite its name, traditional plum pudding contains no actual plums; the term 'plum' historically referred to raisins or other dried fruits.

The pudding's origins can be traced back to medieval English savory dishes, evolving over centuries into the sweet dessert we know today.

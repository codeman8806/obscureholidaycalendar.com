    Overview

    National Raspberry Bombe Day, observed annually on August 11th, celebrates a classic frozen dessert characterized by its spherical or dome shape, typically created in a mold. A raspberry bombe features the sweet-tart flavor of raspberries, often layered with ice cream, sorbet, or mousse, offering a refreshing and elegant treat perfect for summer.

    Why it matters: National Raspberry Bombe Day on August 11 honors a timeless dessert that showcases the vibrant flavor of raspberries in a visually stunning form. This celebration highlights the artistry of frozen confections, inviting food enthusiasts to appreciate the blend of taste and creativity that defines summer indulgence.

    Origin and story

    The term 'bombe glacée' (frozen bombe) originated in France in the 19th century.

    A traditional bombe is made by lining a spherical mold with one type of ice cream or sorbet, filling the center with another flavor or mixture, and then freezing it solid.

      Ways to celebrate

      The "bombe" in this dessert's name is literal — the mold was designed to make the finished dish look like a cannonball.

      Know where the shape comes from

      A bombe glacée is a French frozen dessert built in a spherical mold specifically to resemble a cannonball, with roots tracing back to 16th-century molded frozen desserts in Europe that evolved from earlier Italian techniques.

      Know how the layers actually work

      The mold is lined with a hardened ice cream shell, then filled with "pâte à bombe," a lighter custard-based mixture of whipped cream, syrup, and flavorings that sets without any stirring or churning — a raspberry version typically uses raspberry ice cream or sorbet for that outer shell.

      Did you know?

      The bombe reached its peak of prestige at royal tables — it was served on the menu at Queen Elizabeth II's 1947 wedding to Prince Philip, and legendary chef Auguste Escoffier included more than sixty bombe recipes in his cookbook "Le Guide Culinaire."
